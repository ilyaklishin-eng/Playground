Mass protests: lessons from a decade

This essay reevaluates the 2011 Bolotnaya protests not as a tactical failure, but as a structural inevitability. Framing the defeat as a Hegelian catalyst, it argues the subsequent crackdown inadvertently served as a "civil society university," transforming a naive public into a deeply politicized society primed for an unpredictable historical synthesis.

Likes Go West

In a remarkably prescient May 2014 column for Vedomosti-published more than two full years before the 2016 US presidential election and the ensuing global scandal over Russian cyber interference-Klishin exposed the Kremlin’s nascent digital operations targeting Western democracies. Relying on exclusive sources close to the Presidential Administration, leaked files from "Anonymous International," and insights from European media executives, the piece detailed the strategic pivot of domestic troll farms toward American and European audiences. Klishin dismantled the emerging operational mindset of these state-sponsored networks.

HRW report on harassment of critics includes Klishin case context

This 2012 Human Rights Watch report chronicles the Kremlin’s strategic shift toward targeted pressure on protest organizers. A prime example of this repressive mechanic is the documented episode involving Ilya Klishin, the creator of the "For Fair Elections" network: operatives from the anti-extremism Center "E" interrogated his parents in Tambov, threatening them with vague extremism charges. The text vividly illustrates how authorities, spooked by grassroots mobilization, began deploying psychological blackmail—cynically redirecting their strikes from metropolitan activists to their vulnerable relatives in the provinces.

Reference card for a Human Rights Watch report documenting pressure tactics against protest organizers and their families during the post-2011 crackdown.
